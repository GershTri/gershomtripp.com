Introduction



Getting the Data

The data were collected using the official Yelp Fusion API. Yelp’s API isn’t really made for bulk downloading data, so there were three limits to contend with: 1. max 50 listings per request, 2. max 1000 listings per query (50 listing-chunks x 20 offsets), and 3. max 5000 requests per day. Using district names and PLZ (German ZIP codes) consistently failed because of 2., so I divided up Berlin’s coordinates into a grid of approx. 500x500-meter blocks and sent requests for each block.

The above method worked well, except for one unforeseen problem: Longitudinal coordinates do not run parallel to one another, but converge at the poles and spread as they approach the equator, so the same longitudinal coordinates do not represent the same distances at different latitudes. The upshot was that my grid-scan strategy broke down the more it went east and south. Some sort of correction would be ideal, but I decided for a “good enough” approach and simply shortened the longitudinal intervals and rescanned the affected parts.

This strategy, being more ax than scalpel, generates a lot of duplicates. After removing them I ended up with 11989 unique listings for Berlin and Potsdam, which is a small city just outside of Berlin.

Deciding on a Metric

Ilia Blinderman and Amber Thomas rely on Katie Hempenius’s “incidence rate” metric in her analysis of San Francisco’s neighborhoods. I recommend reading Katie’s post for a better understanding of how she arrived at her metric, but we might summarize her reasoning thusly: Assuming the fraction of categoryz

With the dual goals of “identify[ing] interesting trends” and “normaliz[ing] for the effects of commercial versus residential usage” in mind, Katie arrives at the following formula for “incidence rate”:

\[ X = \frac{Category\;Z\;in\;zip}{Category\;Z\;in\;city} \times \frac{Businesses\;in\;city}{Businesses\;in\;zip} \]

I have a number of problems with this metric, ranging from major to minor.

Some will probably think that my first complaint is just hairsplitting, but think it should be useful, nonetheless. Simply put, the above formula does not describe the incidence rate. The incidence rate is a measure of new cases in a specified period of time. Time, however, doesn’t play a role in the above formula - nor should it, as we are really just interested in a cross-section of Yelp-reviewed Berlin businesses at this point.

This doesn’t at all invalidate Katie’s, Ilia’s, and Amber’s work. “Incidence” is commonly used to indicate the occurence of something. In fact, most dictionary definitions reflect just this usage. Nevertheless, the incidence rate has a more specific meaning than incidence alone, and this is particularly relevant in the present context, where an epidemiological approach is appropriate.

A less pressing issue, but perhaps relevant in terms of computational resources, is the second half of the equation: It is a neighborhood-level constant, and has, as such, no impact on the neighborhood-level rankings, which is what we are interested in. A simplified formula will produce the same results:

\[ X = \frac{Category\;Z\;in\;zip}{Category\;Z\;in\;city} \]

What remains is something that could probably be more accurately described as a riff on the prevalence rate, where our “cases” are the number of businesses from a category found in a given district, and our population consists of all category members. Although useful, there are a number of problems with this metric when it is used alone:

  1. It ignores differences in business frequency. If 10 of 20 city-wide members of category c1 are found in a neighborhood, and 100 of 200 city-wide member of category c2 are found in the same neighborhood, then the formula tells us that the neighborhood is equally characterized by c1 and c2 (10/20 = 0.5 and 100/200 = 0.5). Even worse, if c2 has 210 instead of 200 city-wide members, then the formula tells us that c1 characterizes the neighborhood better than c2, even though c2 occurs 10 times more than c1 in the district!
  2. It is biased in favor of small sample sizes. Different types of businesses likely aren’t evenly distributed across a city. In particular, a small number of niche businesses might more likely be clustered in one area. For example, vegan restaurants might be fairly rare in the city as a whole, but in one particulary trendy neighborhood you might find a cluster of them. As is the nature of things, “trendy” neighborhoods are often also gentrifying inner-city areas with large minority communities. If the minority population is dispersed throughout the rest of the city at a rate greater than the vegan restaurants, then the above formula would suggest that the neighborhood is better characterized as “vegan” than as Black, Latino, Arabic, Indian, etc. even if these communities and cultures are far more prominent in everyday life. Of course, it’s still important to know that there are more vegan restaurants in one neighborhood than anywhere else, but neighborhood’s uniqueness should not be determined primarily by the general scarcity of some of its businesses.
  3. It doesn’t take into account variations in the total number of businesses. Two neighborhoods might both be home to 20 of 100 total category members, i.e. each has 20% of the whole, but neighborhood x has a total of 2,000 businesses, while neighborhood y only has 200. Should we say that the category characterizes both neighborhoods equally? Probably not.
  4. It ignores other indicators of interest. It wouldn’t be farfetched to view the number of listings for a given category in a district as one indication of interest. For example, if we assume that two districts d1 and d2 have, in reality, the same number of businesses from category cx, but there are more Yelp listings for that category in one d1 than d2, then we could hypothesize that people are more interested in cx in d1 than in d2. It would then be reasonable to conclude that cx characterizes d1 more than it does d2. But what if the cx listings in d2 have more reviews on average?

\[ score = prevalence \times proportion \times interest \] Each part of this formula brings its own advantages and disadvantages. For instance, prevalence might tell us what occurs the most, but it doesn’t tell us anything about less prevalent businesses that might still be important in some other way, e.g. in terms of

\[ score = \left( \frac{category_{zip}}{businesses_{zip}} - \frac{category_{city}}{businesses_{city}} \right) \times \frac{category_{zip}}{category_{city}} \times \frac{reviews_{mean/zip}}{reviews_{mean/city}} \]

I’ll refer to the first part as the relative prevalence, since it is a measure of district-level prevalence relative to the city-level prevalence. I’ve chosen to use the relative prevalence, instead of just the district-level prevalence, because it is a better indicator of how the district is different from the city as a whole. It also offers a handy cut-off: Anything less than 0 is less prevalent in the district than in the city, and anything more than 0 is more prevalent in the district than in the city. It’s then just a matter of sorting the relative prevalences from greatest to least to find the categories that are overrepresented in the district.

Dropping categories with small sample sizes (Ilia and Amber set a threshold of 1% of a district’s businesses) ignores the issue posed by 1., i.e. that a neighborhood might be characterized by a small number of unusual businesses, or perhaps a single, very large institution.

I think most of the confusion here stems from the fact that specific epidemiological terminology is being used in a context where an epidemological approach is perfectly valid, but how the terminology is being used isn’t quite right.
